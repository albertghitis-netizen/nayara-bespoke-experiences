Wildlife & Nature

A Birdwatcher's Paradise, and the Toucans Are the Stars

In short: The Arenal rainforest is one of Costa Rica's best birdwatching destinations, with access to more than 900 species. The headliners are the toucans: three of Costa Rica's six species, the keel-billed, the chestnut-mandibled, and the collared aracari, live around Nayara's properties year-round. They do not migrate, so you can see them in any season, and dawn and dusk are best.

Costa Rica is a birdwatcher's paradise, with over 900 species, more than the entire United States.

The Arenal region, where Nayara's resorts are nestled, is particularly renowned for its diversity, from the iconic Resplendent Quetzal to the unmistakable toucan. Arenal sits at the intersection of two major bird migration routes. Every year, millions of birds pass through Central America, and many stop here to feed, rest, and breed. The elevation gradient, from lowland rainforest to cloud forest, creates multiple habitat zones, each with its own bird community.

The result is extraordinary. On a single morning walk you might see 30 or more species; in a full day, experienced birders have recorded over 100. The dawn chorus, the synchronized singing of many species at first light, is one of nature's most magical experiences. But the bird most visitors come hoping to meet is the toucan, so we will start there.

The Headliners

Meeting the Toucans of the Arenal Rainforest

The toucan is one of the most recognizable animals in the world, and travelers come from everywhere to see them, alongside sloths, howler monkeys, scarlet macaws, and red-eyed tree frogs. What many do not know is that Costa Rica is home to six different toucan species, each with its own size, shape, colors, and personality. In the Arenal rainforest, three live all around us: the keel-billed toucan, the chestnut-mandibled toucan, and the collared aracari.

Toucans are omnivores that thrive in hot, dense tropical forests at low elevations, where food is plentiful. They are far more comfortable perching than flying, and tend to hop or glide rather than spend time in the air, which makes these rich forests an ideal home.

A few decades ago, with deforestation sweeping across the region, all six species were under severe threat. But conservation has brought them back, and you can see it on a small scale right here. Much of the land around Nayara Gardens, Springs, and Tented Camp had been turned to pasture; with dedicated reforestation, accelerated by rich volcanic soil and a perfect growing climate, the toucans have returned to the trees around all three resorts. Who They Are

Common Traits of the Arenal Toucans

The most noticeable feature of all toucans is the distinctive beak, which can be up to four times the size of their head. But that is not the only thing that sets them apart. Toucans have strong family and social structures, often traveling in groups of 6 to 20 or more, and even playing games, tossing fruit back and forth in a kind of catch. Parents are attentive, mating monogamously and raising chicks until they can fend for themselves, which takes a while given the toucans' famously awkward movements.

Toucans are often portrayed as elegant and majestic because of their vibrant colors. The reality is that they are rather loud, clumsy, hungry, and confrontational. For those of us who know them well, that adds an endearing goofiness to their behavior.

Why the colorful beak? The size and shape are practical: the bill lets a toucan reach food far out on branches or deep in tree cavities, and its serrated edges handle everything from fruits, nuts, and seeds to other birds' eggs, insects, and small reptiles. The vibrant colors are less settled. Researchers theorize they help attract mates or deter predators; toucans often smack their beak against a branch when threatened, and the color may add to the display.

The Three Species

The Three Arenal Toucans

The keel-billed toucan. One of the most famous and recognizable toucans, thanks to a multi-colored bill that blends just about every color of the rainbow. Keel-billed toucans are very sociable and often group together on branches. You will know they are nearby from their loud, croaking call, which carries up to half a mile.

The chestnut-mandibled toucan. Also called the yellow-fronted toucan, this is the largest toucan in Costa Rica, with a distinctive two-colored bill that sets it apart from the keel-billed's rainbow. Its larger size gives it an even wider diet, adding small snakes and young birds to the menu. Listen for its yelping "yo-YIP!" cry.

The collared aracari. Aracaris look a little different from the toucans most people picture, but they are the same family and share the long beak, diverse diet, and social nature, traveling in groups of 6 to 15. What sets the aracari apart is its acrobatic foraging: it will stretch out on one foot, bend over backward, even hang upside down to reach choice fruit. Its call mixes high-pitched squeaks with lower rattling notes.

How to See Them

How to See Toucans in Arenal

Toucans are all around the rainforest, so you can encounter them on almost any tour or experience. Guests often see them simply walking around the resort, and they have been known to perch right outside the windows of the tents, rooms, and residences. You will find them perched in the trees during the day, or roosting in groups at night to hide from predators.

They are most active at dawn and dusk, so an early-morning or pre-sunset walk is the best time to spot them in their natural habitat. And because toucans do not migrate, they are year-round residents and neighbors, so you can see them in any season. A canopy walk with a naturalist is the surest way; the guides know the fruiting trees the toucans favor.

"At a glance, toucans are majestic, but after getting to know them, you'll see that they're also awkward, goofy, and social, with fascinating personalities and squabbles and games that are incredible to watch. There's always more than meets the eye in the rainforest."

More to See

Beyond the Toucans: Quetzals, Macaws, Hummingbirds

The Resplendent Quetzal. Widely considered one of the most beautiful birds in the world, sacred in Aztec mythology and the national bird of Guatemala. The male is unmistakable: brilliant emerald-green upperparts, a crimson breast, and tail feathers that can extend 24 inches beyond the body. Quetzals live in cloud forest above about 4,000 feet, feeding on wild avocado-like fruits, and depend on undisturbed forest. Seeing one is a privilege among birders.

Macaws and parrots. The region hosts the Scarlet Macaw, the Great Green Macaw, and various parrots, large, colorful, and highly vocal birds that often announce themselves before you see them. Long-lived and intelligent, they usually travel in pairs or small family groups, and disperse seeds that help the forest regenerate.

Hummingbirds. Over 25 species live in Costa Rica, many in the Arenal region. Their wings beat up to 80 times a second, their hearts can exceed 1,000 beats a minute, and they must eat roughly half their body weight in sugar daily. Common local species include the Violet Sabrewing, the Green Hermit, and the Rufous-tailed Hummingbird. Sit quietly near flowering plants or a feeder and wait for them to come to you.

Practical

Tips for Birdwatching at Nayara

Start early. The window from about 5:30 to 8:00 in the morning is the best time, when birds are most active and vocal. Go with a naturalist, who can find and identify what an untrained eye walks past, and bring binoculars and a little patience: sit quietly near flowering or fruiting trees and let the birds come to you. Dawn and dusk reward the early riser and the late strollers alike.

Frequently Asked Questions

How many toucan species live near the Arenal Volcano?+
Three of Costa Rica's six toucan species live in the Arenal rainforest around Nayara's properties: the keel-billed toucan, the chestnut-mandibled toucan, and the collared aracari. They are year-round residents that do not migrate.
When is the best time to see toucans?+
Dawn and dusk, when toucans are most active. Because they do not migrate, they can be seen in any season, so there is no wrong time of year. An early-morning or pre-sunset walk, ideally with a naturalist, gives the best chance.
Why do toucans have such large, colorful beaks?+
The size lets a toucan reach food far out on branches or deep in tree cavities, and serrated edges let it eat fruit, nuts, seeds, eggs, insects, and even small reptiles. The bright colors are less settled scientifically; researchers think they may help attract mates or deter predators.
How many bird species can you see around Arenal?+
Costa Rica has over 900 bird species, more than the entire United States, and the Arenal region is among the richest, sitting at the intersection of two migration routes. On a single morning walk you might see 30 or more species; a full day can yield over 100. Beyond toucans, highlights include the Resplendent Quetzal, scarlet and great green macaws, and more than 25 hummingbird species.
Do I need a guide to go birdwatching?+
Not strictly, since toucans and many birds can be seen right around the resorts, but a naturalist greatly improves what you see and identify. Guides know the fruiting trees the toucans favor and the calls that give each species away, which turns a walk into a far richer experience.
