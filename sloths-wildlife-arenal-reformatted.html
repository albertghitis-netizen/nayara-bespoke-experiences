Part Two

The Wildlife Is the Trip

In short: The sloths in the Arenal rainforest around Nayara are wild, not caged. Decades of replanting brought the forest back across former pasture, and the sloths returned on their own when the canopy closed. For a family, that is the heart of the trip: a child finding a real, free-living sloth from a canopy walk, alongside toucans, howler monkeys, and coatis, with a naturalist to help spot them.

The four-year-old usually sees it first. A sloth, motionless and moss-covered, invisible until you know where to look.

The single thing children remember from Arenal is the animals, and the single thing that makes the animals worth the trip is that they are wild. They are not in an enclosure, there are no feeding times, and there is no gift shop at the exit. The forest was rebuilt, and the wildlife moved back in on its own. The animals are home. You are the guest.

The Context

Why the Forest Is This Alive

Costa Rica's wildlife is the product of geography. When the Isthmus of Panama closed roughly three million years ago, it joined two continents that had each evolved their own animals in isolation, and the species poured across the new land bridge in both directions. What remained was a narrow strip of land with a disproportionate share of the world's biological complexity. Costa Rica, roughly the size of West Virginia, holds an estimated 500,000 species, more biodiversity than most continents.

The Arenal Conservation Area alone spans more than 500,000 acres, climbing from lowland rainforest through cloud forest, a vertical cross-section of that complexity along the volcano's slopes. It is why a single canopy walk can turn up a sloth, three kinds of toucan, and a troop of howler monkeys inside an hour. The density is real, and it is the reason the wildlife is the trip.

Restoration

How the Sloths Came Back

Much of the land beneath the wider Arenal estate was cleared cattle pasture when Nayara acquired it. Open, exposed, functionally empty: no canopy, no food web, no connection to the surrounding rainforest. The decision early on was that this would not be a project built on degraded land. It would be a project that reversed it.

The work began with species selection. Fast-establishing native trees rebuilt canopy quickly. Cecropia became foundational: it colonizes disturbed land rapidly and produces the leaves sloths depend on. As the canopy closed, structure returned. Shade stabilized the understory, insects reappeared, and movement corridors formed overhead.

The sloths came back on their own. That is the only outcome that matters. Two species now live here year-round, monitored by resident naturalists. The sloth is not a symbol; it is a living measure of whether the forest is working. See how it became the clearest indicator of regeneration in the soul of Nayara.

For Families

Finding Sloths with Children

Sloths are wild, so they are never guaranteed, but a few things stack the odds. Go with a naturalist: a trained guide with a spotting scope finds in minutes what most guests walk straight past, and lets a child see the animal up close through the lens without going near it. Mornings and late afternoons are best, when sloths shift position to feed. Look for a dark, shaggy clump high in a cecropia tree, the leaves they love most.

Make it a game. Children spot sloths better than adults because they are patient about looking up and have no fixed idea of what to expect. Give them the job of finding the next one and the walk becomes theirs. The guided canopy walk, on bridges that bring you level with the treetops, is the single best place to do it.

The same outing reaches the rest of the cast, and the gentle hanging-bridge walks double as the family's adventure morning. See how that fits the wider week in part three, family adventures around the Arenal Volcano.

The Cast

The Other Residents: Toucans, Howlers, Coatis

The sloth gets the headline, but the supporting cast is what makes the forest feel alive, and each one is doing real work.

The toucan family. Keel-billed, chestnut-mandibled, and collared aracari toucans move through the mid-canopy eating large fruits most birds cannot. As documented by the Cornell Lab of Ornithology, they are among the most effective seed dispersers in Neotropical forests, deciding where the next generation of trees grows. They are also the easiest showstopper to spot at breakfast. Meet them up close in meeting the toucans of the Arenal rainforest.

The mantled howler monkey. Howlers are the alarm clock of the rainforest, their dawn calls carrying for over a kilometer. They cannot safely cross open ground, so the planted canopy corridors are, among other things, howler-monkey infrastructure. Their morning chorus is the sound children remember.

The white-nosed coati. Raccoon relatives with long, ringed tails, coatis move through the undergrowth in busy troops, turning soil and dispersing seeds. Children love them because they are active, curious, and out in daylight, the animal most likely to be doing something rather than sleeping.

The Right Way

Seeing It Without Disturbing It

Wild animals stay wild only if they are watched well. Resident naturalists guide the wildlife outings, keeping observation distance, timing, and behavior calibrated to each species, so a sloth is never crowded and a nesting bird is never flushed. It is also the best way to actually see things: the guide knows the trees the sloths favor and the hours the toucans feed.

For children, watching well is half the lesson. They learn that you do not touch, you do not feed, and you keep your distance, and that the reward for patience is a real animal living its real life a few meters away. That is a different thing entirely from a petting zoo, and they feel the difference.

A sloth does not care about our intentions. The only thing it responds to is whether the conditions for life are present or absent.

Frequently Asked Questions

Can you really see sloths in the Arenal rainforest?+
Often, yes, though they are wild and never guaranteed. Two sloth species live year-round in the restored canopy around Nayara, and guests regularly spot them from the trails and on guided canopy walks. They are not kept in an enclosure; the reforestation brought them back and they stayed. A naturalist with a spotting scope greatly improves your odds.
What is the best time of day to spot wildlife?+
Early morning and late afternoon are best. Sloths shift position to feed at those hours, howler monkeys call at dawn, and toucans are most active feeding in the cooler parts of the day. Midday heat tends to quiet the forest.
How did the sloths return to Nayara's grounds?+
Through the reforestation program. Sloths depend on continuous canopy cover and specific food sources, especially cecropia leaves. As the planted trees matured and the canopy closed, the conditions they require returned. They were not introduced; they moved in on their own when the forest was ready.
Is wildlife viewing safe and suitable for young children?+
Yes. Guided canopy walks and trail outings are gentle and well suited to children, and a naturalist tailors the pace and length to the group. Observation is from a distance, which keeps both the animals and the children safe, and children often spot wildlife faster than the adults.
What other animals might we see besides sloths?+
Commonly toucans (keel-billed, chestnut-mandibled, and collared aracari), mantled howler monkeys, white-nosed coatis, and a wide range of birds and butterflies, including the blue morpho. Sightings vary by day and season, which is part of why a wild forest is more rewarding than an enclosure.
