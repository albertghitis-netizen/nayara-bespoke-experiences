This is the multi-generational and family guest, not the honeymoon couple the page currently speaks to.

Is family the right target based on volume?

Yes, but not because it is the biggest number. It is the right target because it is the rare cluster that is winnable, on-product, and the fix for everything else.

On raw volume, family (~4,500) is smaller than the head terms (22,200) or all-inclusive (49,500). But those bigger numbers are not catchable: the head terms are owned by listicles, and all-inclusive is the wrong intent. Family is winnable (no rival owns it in Arenal), it matches our actual product and our actual audience, and fixing the family positioning lifts conversion across all of Gardens' traffic, including the brand searches that already arrive. So we target family as the positioning spine, paired with the larger Arenal and La Fortuna geo terms for volume, with sloths as the hook. That combination is bigger and more winnable than chasing any single head term.

One measurement note: because Gardens barely appears for family today, Search Console cannot size the demand yet. The Semrush US volumes above are our best estimate, and we will confirm with Keyword Planner now that measurement is connected. We treat the family page as a test we prove, not a promise.

4. The combined Family + Sloth page

You combined the family page and the sloth page. That is exactly right, and here is why and how.

Can we combine family and sloths into one page?

Yes. They are stronger together than apart.

Family is the search demand (the keywords people type). Sloths are the reason to believe and the emotional hook (the thing kids beg for, the thing no competitor can copy, and already the answer AI assistants give for "hotel with sloths"). Sloths have almost no direct search volume, so a standalone sloth page would rank for nothing; folded into the family page, the sloth becomes the headline that makes the family page convert and earns the AI and social attention.

How to structure the one page

  • Target family in the page mechanics: title, H1, URL, and headings built around the family terms (family resort in Arenal, Costa Rica with kids). This is what Google reads.
  • Lead with the sloth as the hero: the on-property sloth trail and sanctuary as the opening promise, packaged for families ("the easiest place in Costa Rica for your kids to see a sloth").
  • Prove the family product: suites that sleep four, guaranteed adjacent rooms, babysitting, on-call doctor, stroller-friendly paths, kids' programming. Verified claims only.
  • Add FAQ structured data for both stories: "Is Nayara Gardens good for families?", "Do rooms sleep four?", "Can my kids see sloths?", "Is it all-inclusive?" This is what wins the AI answers Gardens is absent from.
  • Credit the sloths to Gardens, correcting the drift that currently attributes the sloth sanctuary to Springs.
  • "Check availability" as the call to action, with a clean, migration-ready URL.

Is "luxury glamping" luxury enough?

Yes. Tented commands the highest booking value of all three properties ($5,978) and is the top revenue performer, so the market is already paying a premium for it.

"Glamping" as a word can sound down-market, but Tented's product is the opposite: 1,700 square foot tents with private plunge pools and volcano views, rated Travel + Leisure number two in the world. The data backs the position: Tented owns the glamping searches, gets discovered for "luxury resorts costa rica" on Maps, and is our strongest social account (90-day reach of 143,773). The right move is not to abandon "glamping," which is where the demand is and where Tented dominates, but to always pair it with the luxury proof points: "luxury tented camp," private plunge pools, the awards. Glamping is the category; luxury is the qualifier. Tented owns both.

Why this matters for Gardens: keeping Springs on romance and Tented on luxury glamping leaves family wholly to Gardens. That clean separation is what makes the family position defensible and stops the three properties from blurring into each other in search and AI answers.

6. How the corporate site and Gardens interact

We run two separate websites that need to cooperate, not compete: nayararesorts.com (corporate) and nayaragardens.com (the property).

What each does

  • nayaragardens.com is the property site where the booking happens. It should own the Gardens-brand and family content (the family and sloth page lives here).
  • nayararesorts.com is the brand hub and the home for non-brand category pages (Best Luxury Resorts in Costa Rica, a "Which Nayara is right for you" comparison, an Arenal and La Fortuna page). These target general searches and funnel the right guest to Gardens.

How they pass guests between each other

Analytics shows the corporate site already sends visitors and bookings to Gardens (a nayararesorts.com referral into Gardens), and Gardens hands completed bookings to the reservation engine. Two issues come with that:

  • They can compete on the Gardens name. The corporate site also ranks for "nayara gardens," so putting Gardens-brand pages on the corporate domain would split the name between two of our own sites. That is why Gardens-brand content stays on nayaragardens.com for now.
  • The handoff hides attribution. The corporate site drew 153,839 visits in 90 days but records almost no bookings on itself, because the booking happens after the handoff. So the value of corporate and social effort is real but currently hard to prove. Fixing cross-domain tracking is what makes it visible.
The plan: build the non-brand feeder pages on corporate (positioning Gardens as the family pick), keep the family and sloth page on nayaragardens.com, cross-link them cleanly, and fix cross-domain tracking. Full consolidation of the two sites is a later project, gated behind the conversion-tag fixes already underway.
