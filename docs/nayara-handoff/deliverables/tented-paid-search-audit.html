Executive Summary

The account defends the brand well and buys almost nothing else on purpose

All-time, the account has spent $348,804 for 4,900 conversions at a blended $71.18 per booking across 12.9M impressions. Underneath that average sit two very different machines. Brand search converts at $41 to $62 per booking. Nearly everything else is either bleeding (a broad-luxury ad group running at $165 per booking), invisible (the glamping category queries Nayara should own get ~150 impressions a year), or unbuilt (romance and honeymoon intent effectively does not exist in the account).

Three key findings

1. One ad group accounts for the leak. The two Luxury‑Market RSAs spent $70,380 for 425 bookings, a blended $165.48 against the account's own non-brand benchmark of $60.23. The excess is roughly $44,764, or 743 bookings foregone at the same spend.

2. Copy is not the variable; query mix is. Fifteen RSAs reduce to three near-identical recipes cloned across four geos. The same headline set converts at $41 in one cell and $247 in another. Asset-level "winners" and "losers" largely inherit the traffic they were placed in.

3. The growth pillars are already validated. The brand's own search terms show intense, unconverted family-tent demand and confirmed spa intent; industry data ranks family, multigenerational, adventure, and wellness travel as 2026's top luxury motivations; and the honeymoon cluster, the strongest brand fit of all, is untouched.

The recommended structure rebuilds Search into five pillars (Brand Defense, Luxury Glamping, Destination Intent, Romance/Honeymoon, Wellness Retreat), funded entirely by reallocating the Luxury‑Market budget, with family handled first as a landing-page fix and a deliberate positioning decision rather than a paid bet.

How to Read the Data

One metric decides, the rest is context

Cost per conversion is the decision metric throughout this report, with one critical qualifier: where it is measured. At the ad level, conversions are credited to whole ads, so the numbers are load-bearing. At the asset level, Google distributes credit across everything that served together, which is why high-volume sitelinks, callouts, and even the business logo all cluster suspiciously near the account average ($59 to $61): they inherit campaign performance rather than drive it. Asset cost-per-conversion is only meaningful when comparing the same asset type, in the same campaign type, at meaningful volume.

Two metrics should be actively distrusted. Interaction rate and CPM are not comparable across campaign types (Performance Max long headlines post 70%+ interaction rates as a placement artifact, not great copy). And Ad Strength grades checklist compliance, not outcomes: the account's only "Excellent" search ad is its single worst performer at $173.83 per booking, while the "Average" ads are the best converters.

One data caveat applies to every all-time figure: 33% of historical spend is invisible in the enabled-ads exports. Removed and paused ads carry $116,239 of spend and 1,640 conversions ($60,293 of it in deleted Search ads). Any all-time conclusion here is drawn from two-thirds of the history; future pulls should include removed ads or use fixed date ranges.

Finding 01 · The Leak

Luxury‑Market: cheap clicks, expensive bookings

The largest ad in the account by spend is a Luxury‑Market RSA: $55,234, 1.91M impressions, a seductive $0.56 CPC, a 0.32% conversion rate, and $173.83 per booking. Its sibling ran $15,146 at $140.82 (0.26% conversion rate). Both sit inside the campaign labeled "Brand," which means 2.7M impressions of broad "luxury hotel" traffic have been inflating brand reporting while converting at a fifth the rate of true brand queries. Interaction rates tell the story instantly: 5% on Luxury‑Market versus 26 to 32% on the brand ad group next door.

Finding 02 · Creative

Fifteen RSAs, three ads: copy is not the variable

Headline-set similarity analysis across all 15 RSAs shows the Search account is three copy recipes rotated across US, Canada, Europe, and Latam. Two Europe/Latam pairs are 100% identical; the $44k US brand ad is a 90% match to a Canada ad; the failing $55k Luxury‑Market ad shares 64 to 83% of its headlines with brand ads converting at $41 to $48.

Same words, five-fold performance spread: the variable is query mix and geography, not creative. Two consequences follow. First, asset-report verdicts are entangled with placement ("Leading Hotels of the World" at $114.10 and "Feel enriched by a paradisal escape to the Costa Rican Forest" at $118.83 look weak largely because they live inside Luxury‑Market traffic; do not kill assets on asset numbers alone). Where asset comparisons are clean (same type, same campaign type, real volume), the spread is still actionable: "Luxury tents in the rainforest" converts at $40.88 (2.48% conv rate) against "Rustic Elegance in Costa Rica" at $97.34 across 132k impressions; the description "A Luxury Tented Camp nestled in the Costa Rica Rainforest" at $28.42 against the high-volume "Indulge in ultimate glamping luxury" at $65.52. Concrete place-and-product language wins the next copy refresh.

Finding 03 · Structure

Pinning monoculture, and what Ad Strength actually measures

Thirteen of fifteen RSAs pin "Nayara Tented Camp" to position 1, and Google flags every one of them with the same improvement note ("add unique headlines or unpin some assets"). The only two unpinned ads in the account are the ones rated Excellent and Good, and one of them is the worst performer in the entire account. Ad Strength rewarded checklist compliance on $55k of waste while grading the $41 to $48 workhorses "Average."

Move: treat Ad Strength as decorative. Pinning the brand name on brand queries is defensible; the real cost is unmeasured rotation. Run one unpinned variant per major ad group as a controlled test rather than unpinning wholesale.

Finding 04 · Brand Quality

A typo has ~$10.5k of media behind it

"Nestled in a magnificiant ancient jungle. Book your stay now." is live in 6 of 18 enabled ads, including the two biggest spenders (the $55k Luxury‑Market RSA and the Display ad), with roughly $10,480 of all-time spend behind the line across Search, Performance Max, and Display. The Performance Max asset pool also contains "Family-firendly Glamping" ($348 spend). For a Leading Hotels of the World property, both are same-day fixes.

Finding 05 · Measurement & Hygiene

Tracking is bare, and it connects to the GA4 gap

Every single ad in the account has an empty tracking template, empty final URL suffix, and no custom parameters; display paths are unused. Attribution therefore rides entirely on auto-tagging, so any gclid loss (consent mode, redirects, iOS) lands in GA4 as direct or organic traffic. This is plausibly a measurable share of the GA4-versus-Ads reconciliation gap already on the roadmap. The "Platform Comparable" conversion columns exist in the export but are completely empty, meaning cross-platform comparison was never configured.

Related hygiene from the same pulls: the Display campaign is live at $10,811 with zero attributed conversions (awareness value unmeasured rather than disproven, but it deserves a deliberate review); Demand Gen's $8,837 is historical, its campaign is paused; and the search-term data shows ~300 impressions of sister-property queries ("nayara resort spa & gardens" variants) hitting the Tented Camp account, plus a long tail of competitor-name matches (Tabacón, The Springs, Westin Conchal) at near-zero cost that should be either negatived out or turned into a deliberate conquesting test, not left as an accidental middle.

Move: adopt an account-wide URL suffix standard (UTMs for source/medium/campaign/ad group), route Gardens-intent queries to the correct property, and make the competitor-term decision explicitly.

Theme Economics

Concrete hooks, thematic proof

Aggregating every family-themed and wellness-themed asset in the account reveals one consistent law, the same one luxury copy obeys: themes convert as descriptions (reassurance) and fail as headlines (the hook). Family and wellness currently exist only as Performance Max asset flavoring plus spa sitelinks; neither has a Search lane.

Writing rule for everything that follows: headlines name the concrete thing (brand, tents, rainforest, volcano, open-air spa, private hot springs); descriptions carry the theme (wellness, family, romance). Abstract aspiration as a hook has now failed three times in this account's own data.

Market Validation

Are these the right areas? The market says yes, loudly

The Virtuoso 2026 Luxe Report ranks immediate-family travel as the single biggest luxury trend for 2026, multigenerational travel second, and active/adventure travel third, with wellness now the second-most requested experience for solo clients and treated as a core motivation rather than a niche. Industry research adds one execution warning that matches this account's data exactly: generic spa positioning no longer sells; travelers want specific, named outcomes. Lead with the open-air rainforest spa and the hot springs, not the word "wellness."

Verdicts by pillar: Brand, obviously yes. Luxury, right area, wrong version; the defensible territory is luxury glamping, the category Nayara functionally owns in Costa Rica ($50 to $66 across comparable assets). Wellness, yes, promoted from garnish to a small dedicated lane. Family, converts cheaply and tops the trend charts, but remains a positioning decision against the brand's adults-luxury line; it must be chosen deliberately, not run by default inside PMax. Romance/Honeymoon and Adventure/Wildlife, the two unbuilt pillars, have stronger evidence than half of what is currently funded: the sloth sanctuary is the most differentiating asset the property owns and is currently used as a callout rather than a campaign.

The category king is invisible on category queries

Across every Costa Rica glamping query in twelve months ("glamping costa rica" 41 impressions, "costa rica glamping" 39, "luxury glamping costa rica" 10, "best glamping in costa rica" 6, "arenal glamping" 7, and the rest of the tail), the account captured roughly 150 impressions total. These match by accident, not targeting. Meanwhile "nayara tented camp spa" drew 428 impressions at a 34% CTR, confirming spa intent on brand, while generic wellness queries ("costa rica wellness resorts," "best wellness hotels in costa rica") show one to two impressions each: untargeted, not unwanted.

Romance is a ghost

"Nayara tented camp honeymoon" appeared 11 times all year, plus single impressions for "costa rica resorts for couples" and similar. The honeymoon cluster is not underperforming; it does not exist in the account. Absence of impressions here measures absence of targeting, not absence of demand, which is why the cluster gets a sized test in the restructure.

The Restructure

Five pillars of Search

Budget shares apply to the monthly Search budget. Pillars 4 and 5 together cost less than Luxury‑Market was burning: this is reallocation, not new money.

1 · Brand Defense

~35% of budget · CPA target $45 to $60

Exact-match Nayara terms only, permanently separated from non-brand. Sister-property ("Spa & Gardens") queries routed to the correct property. One unpinned test variant; a family-tent sitelink added once the landing page exists. This is the engine; the job is to keep it uncontaminated.

2 · Luxury Glamping (category ownership)

~20 to 25% · CPA target $60 to $75 · Goal: 80%+ impression share on ~15 core terms

The glamping-Costa Rica cluster the brand should own outright and currently sees ~150 impressions a year on. Funded by the old Luxury‑Market budget: same money, queries Nayara can actually win. Copy already proven: "Luxury Glamping" converts at $50.51, "Costa Rica Luxury Glamping" at $65.83.

3 · Destination Intent

~15 to 20% · CPA target $60 to $75

Scale what the BestResort ad group already proved at $60.23 and a 1.63% conversion rate: arenal volcano hotels, la fortuna luxury hotels, best resort costa rica. Absorbs the migrated Luxury‑Market keywords under their own tROAS.

4 · Romance / Honeymoon (new build)

~10% test budget · Test gate $90 · Scale gate $75

Costa Rica honeymoon and couples clusters, landing on the romance content already produced this quarter. Highest brand fit in the portfolio, zero current presence, so it gets a clean sized test instead of a guess.

5 · Wellness Retreat

~10% · Test gate $90 · Scale gate $75

Retreat- and spa-resort-intent terms landing on /unwind/. Concrete hooks only (open-air rainforest spa, private hot springs, thermal water), theme in the descriptions, per the $55-versus-$115 split in the account's own data.

Family: fix before funding

Family stays out of paid non-brand for now. Sequence: build or fix the family-tent landing path (the demand and the leak are both proven), add the family sitelink to brand, settle the adults-luxury positioning question deliberately, and only then promote family to a sixth pillar with the "luxury family resort costa rica" cluster. Adventure/wildlife (the sloth sanctuary) is the natural seventh candidate after that: "Adventure Awaits in Costa Rica" already converts at $44.73.
