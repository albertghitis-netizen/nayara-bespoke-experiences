Why Costa Rica Feels the Way It Does

In short: Costa Rica ranks near the top of the Happy Planet Index because nature exposure, time affluence, dense social ties, and a sense of purpose overlap here more than almost anywhere. Nayara does not manufacture that wellbeing; it removes the friction between you and the conditions Costa Rica already provides.

On April 7, the World Health Organization observes World Health Day.

This year's theme, Together for health. Stand with science, asks a question we think about every day: what actually makes people well? Not what treats illness. What produces health in the first place. We live and work in a country that has been quietly answering that question for decades.

There are countries that feel efficient, countries that feel rich, countries that feel beautiful, and countries that feel alive. Costa Rica belongs to the last category. You notice it before you understand it. The air feels softer. Meals take longer. Forest is never very far away. People do not move as if every hour is under attack. What strikes visitors first is not luxury, not even nature, but a strange reduction in friction. Life feels less clenched.

That feeling has a name, and like most things tourists repeat too easily, it is usually flattened by repetition. Pura Vida appears on mugs, airport shirts, surf shops, and hotel walls. It is in danger of becoming decorative. But cliches only form around ideas that once had force. Pura Vida survived because it still does.

On paper, Costa Rica does not look like a country that should feel richer in life than nations with many times its wealth. And yet it often does. That is not an illusion. Costa Rica has consistently placed in the top tier of the Happy Planet Index, which combines wellbeing, life expectancy, inequality of outcomes, and ecological footprint. The question is what Costa Rica understands about health that richer countries often seem to forget.

The Decision

The Decision That Changed the Atmosphere

To understand why Costa Rica feels the way it does, you have to begin with a decision so unusual that it still sounds improbable. In 1949, Costa Rica constitutionally abolished its army. UNESCO's Memory of the World listing treats the abolition as documentary heritage and frames it as part of the country's long-term commitment to peace and social development.

Over time, that choice compounded into a broader civic atmosphere. Costa Rica built high literacy, invested in public health and education, and protected a significant share of its land. The World Bank literacy indicator places Costa Rica in the mid-90s, while the OECD's 2025 country note places terrestrial protected areas at roughly 26% of national territory.

That is why Costa Rica feels different. Not because it escaped modernity, but because it made certain tradeoffs differently. The result is a country where health often feels embedded in the landscape and tempo of life rather than outsourced entirely to institutions that only intervene once something has already gone wrong.

The Science

What the Science Actually Suggests

The strongest explanation for Costa Rica's appeal is not a single secret. It is an overlap of known variables that wellbeing researchers have been circling for years. Taken individually, none of them is uniquely Costa Rican. Taken together, they explain why the country feels the way it does.

Start with what the body already knows. A widely cited Frontiers in Psychology report on the "nature pill" study found that spending 20 to 30 minutes in a natural setting was associated with a meaningful reduction in cortisol, the body's primary stress hormone. Costa Rica's advantage is that nature exposure is unusually hard to avoid.

Then there is time. Research summarized by Harvard Business School on time affluence argues that the feeling of having enough time is a major predictor of wellbeing, in some contexts more important than additional income once basic needs are met.

Underneath both is the thickness of social life. The Harvard Study of Adult Development, the most famous long-running study of human flourishing, has a central finding that has not changed: close relationships predict health and happiness better than status, money, or achievement.

And then there is what the Blue Zones profile of Nicoya calls plan de vida, a reason to get up in the morning. That phrase matters because it is modest. It is not the language of optimization or elite performance. It is the language of coherence.

The Phrase

Pura Vida Is Not Translation. It Is Compression.

Tourists often ask what Pura Vida means as if the answer were lexical. It is not. Literal translations are easy. Pure life. Simple life. Good life. None of them quite gets it. Pura Vida is not just a phrase but a compression of attitude, permission, and proportion. It is what a culture says when it refuses to make stress sound noble.

That is why the phrase matters more than it should. It names something wealthy societies regularly lose: the ability to experience enoughness without reading it as failure. That does not make Costa Rica lazy, unserious, or untouched by ambition. It makes it unusually unwilling to glorify tension for its own sake. And that may be one of the healthiest things about it.

The Foundation

The Ecosystem That Makes It Possible

Everything in this article depends on something staying alive. The forest that lowers cortisol only works if the forest is there. The water that lowers vigilance only flows if the watershed is intact. The birds whose calls stitch the canopy together only sing where the canopy is continuous. A place that feels alive changes the person inside it. Not through metaphor. Through physiology.

The same living systems that sustain wildlife are the ones that restore people. That connection is the reason we treat conservation not as a corporate initiative but as the foundation of everything we offer.

Pura Vida is not something a resort can teach.

At best, it can create the quiet in which you finally notice it.
