The Short Answer

Is Costa Rica an All-Inclusive Destination?

In short: Costa Rica has a handful of all-inclusive resorts, mostly beach properties in Guanacaste, but it is not really an all-inclusive destination, and that is its strength. Almost every hotel, including Nayara Gardens, includes breakfast and lets you choose the rest. If you want a true all-inclusive within the Nayara collection, two resorts offer it, both outside Costa Rica: Nayara Bocas del Toro in Panama and Nayara Alto Atacama in Chile.

Yes, you can book an all-inclusive resort in Costa Rica. The honest question is whether you should.

A small set of all-inclusive resorts operates in the country, concentrated on the Guanacaste coast. They are real, and for some trips they are the right call. But roughly 98% of Costa Rica's accommodations are not all-inclusive, and the country's appeal, the wildlife, the volcanoes, the small towns and family-run restaurants, lives largely outside resort walls.

So this guide does two things. First, it answers plainly what is and is not included at the kind of resort most travelers are actually considering. Then it makes the case for the way most seasoned Costa Rica travelers go: resort-quality comfort without the all-inclusive wristband.

What's Included

What Is Actually Included, and Where

At a true all-inclusive, your rate bundles the room, all meals, drinks, and most on-site activities and entertainment into one price. In Costa Rica, those resorts are mostly on the Guanacaste beaches and suit travelers who want a fixed cost and a stay-put beach holiday.

Nayara's three Costa Rica resorts in the Arenal region, Nayara Gardens, Nayara Springs, and Nayara Tented Camp, are not all-inclusive. The nightly rate includes a full breakfast, the rainforest setting, and the resort's grounds, while dining beyond breakfast, spa treatments, and guided experiences are arranged as you go. That is deliberate: it keeps you free to explore the volcano, the waterfalls, and the town of La Fortuna rather than eating every meal behind one gate.

If a fully all-inclusive Nayara is what you want, two resorts offer it, and both are outside Costa Rica: Nayara Bocas del Toro in Panama, an overwater private island, and Nayara Alto Atacama in Chile, a desert lodge with guided excursions built in.

The Case

Why Costa Rica Rewards Going Beyond All-Inclusive

Every traveler wants the same three things from a holiday: to relax, to enjoy it, and to feel it was worth the money. An all-inclusive can deliver all three. In Costa Rica, going beyond one usually delivers more of each.

On value, the all-inclusive math only works if you use everything you have prepaid. Many of Costa Rica's non-all-inclusive hotels already fold in the things you actually want, breakfast, hot springs, kayaks or bikes, so you pay for what you use and skip the casino and tennis court you were never going to touch. On experience, the country's best moments are the wild sloth on the trail, the soda restaurant in town, the guide who grew up beside the volcano. Those happen when you are free to move, not bound to a single property's schedule and stage show.

None of this requires roughing it or speaking Spanish or riding a chicken bus. Costa Rica is developed, welcoming, and easy to travel. Skipping the all-inclusive label is not a downgrade. For most travelers, it is the upgrade.

No Trade-Off

You Do Not Sacrifice Resort Quality

The fear behind booking all-inclusive is usually this: that the alternative means roughing it. In Costa Rica, it does not. The country is full of resort-quality stays, gorgeous properties on its best beaches and sustainable luxury lodges hidden in the rainforest, that simply price dining and experiences separately.

Nayara Gardens is the clearest example. It is the family flagship of the Nayara collection and the most-reviewed of the three Nayara resorts in Costa Rica, with suites that sleep four, volcanic hot-spring access, wild sloths on the trails, and Michelin-recognized dining nearby at the adults-only Nayara Springs. Breakfast is included; the rest is yours to choose. Nothing about that is roughing it.

The real move is to redefine all-inclusive for yourself: not a wristband, but everything Costa Rica includes when you are free to go and find it.

If You Want the Package

If You Still Want All-Inclusive, Nayara Has It

There are good reasons to want a true all-inclusive: a young family that does not want to think about a bill at every meal, a once-a-year escape where the point is to not plan. The Nayara collection has two, and they are among the most striking all-inclusive resorts anywhere.

Nayara Bocas del Toro, Panama. An adults-only private island of overwater villas in the Caribbean, all-inclusive by design, so dining, drinks, and most experiences are simply part of the stay.

Nayara Alto Atacama, Chile. A desert lodge in the Atacama where the all-inclusive rate folds in daily guided excursions across salt flats, geysers, and high-altitude lagoons, plus an on-site observatory under some of the clearest skies on Earth.

A favorite itinerary pairs the two ideas: the rainforest, a la carte, at Nayara Gardens in Costa Rica, then a fully all-inclusive stretch in Panama or Chile.

Decide

How to Decide for Your Trip

Choose all-inclusive if you want a fixed cost, a stay-put beach holiday, and as little planning as possible. In Costa Rica that points you to the Guanacaste coast; within Nayara it points to Bocas del Toro or Alto Atacama.

Go beyond all-inclusive if you came for Costa Rica itself, the wildlife, the volcano, the towns and the food, and want resort comfort without being confined to one property. That is Nayara Gardens, Springs, or Tented Camp in Arenal.
